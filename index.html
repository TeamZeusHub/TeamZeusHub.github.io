about

Here at Team Zeus, we are all about electric motorcycle racing. We were founded in 2011 by a group of ambitious students from the University of Calgary, and have recently finished building and racing our very own ‘Zephyr’ at the emotoRacing competition in Utah this September.

Electrical Team

The team that designs and prototypes energy storage solutions such as battery packs as well as test and program our data acquisition equipment.

Mechanical Team

The Mechanical Team works on a mini electric motorcycle prototype and fabricates the frame for the next generation electric motorcycle.

Business Team

Our budgeting and accounting team will be responsible for determining project costs, projecting our income, and keeping track of our expenditures; and the sponsorship team takes our proposal to corporations and grant providing institutions.

External Team

One pillar of our club is not only to enhance the reputation of Team ZEUS at a university level, but also the reputation of the University of Calgary in the international community.

Internal Team

Human resources, leadership development, safety programs as well as technical development are taken care of by the internal activities team.

The Zephyr

The Zephyr is Team ZEUS’ first ever racing bike. With Tim Johnson as the pilot, it raced in August, 2014, at the eMotoRacing competition in Tooele, Utah.

Frame

Modified 2004 Suzuki GSX-R 600

Motor

Motenergy 1302

Power

40 HP (30 kW)

Torque

46.5 ft-lbs (63 Nm)

Weight (Wet)

460 lbs

Top Speed

130.7 km/h

Estimated Range

50 km


We started building the Zephyr in early 2013 with the purchase of our motorcycle frame. The Zephyr began life as a 2004 Suzuki GSX-R 600 which had a a fast and furious past as a gasoline powered racing bike with Calgary’s Motorcycle Roadracing Association (CMRA). We removed all the gasoline components and were left with a lightweight aluminum frame, complete with race quality suspension.

The Mule

Team ZEUS’ first bike

Top Speed

~20 km/h

Range

untested

Voltage

48 V

Max Amperage

40 A

Horsepower

~1.5 hp


Lovingly dubbed “The Mule” this motorcycle was Team Zeus’ humble beginnings. While far from a race-worthy bike, the aim here was to convert an existing gasoline motorcycle to electric using a budget of under $1000 and as little outside help as possible. We managed to successfully pull it off, producing a fully functional electric motorcycle and learning a wealth of knowledge about how they work.

In the second phase of the project, we are upgrading the very underpowered Mule with a much more powerful motor that we are building from scratch. Using a slightly modified blueprint from a detailed ebook, the “Axel Borg Motor” should theoritically allow the Mule to hit highway speeds.

As for future plans, the Mule will always remain as a project bike to tinker around on and for new generations of first years to learn on.

Axel Borg Motor

Spinny wheely thingy

Approximate Weight

10 kg

Dimensions

366 mm x 120 mm

Voltage

48 V

Max Amperage

375 A

Power Output

7 kW avg, 18 kW peak


After completing the mule, and after having come to the conclusion that we wanted to design our own motor for the coming race bike, we came across the Axel Borg Motor and decided to built it, in order to learn what building an electric motor is all about. The design is based on the work of an engineer named Axel Borg (how cool a name is that?) out of Sweden. We purchased a detailed ebook from his website and after some slight modifications, we set to work.

The initial design was an axial flux, AC, 3-phase, salient coil, permanent magnet motor that could be easily built from readily available materials, but still produce practical levels of performance. The original motor, primarily out of wood in a backyard shop, was powerful enough to be installed in a small motorcycle and hit 90km/h. Here are the initial promised specs, according to the book

Since we had access to a machine shop, we decided to use some more advanced materials, so we chose to go with aluminum for the rotors, due to lightweight, ease of machineability and its nonmagnetic nature, along with phenolic for for the stator. We also chose to go with higher grade N48 Neodymium magnets, rather than the suggested N45 magnets, since the difference in price was small. In addition to these being stronger materials than wood, we also no longer had to worry about warping and could hit much finer tolerances, allowing us to decrease the motor air-gaps, thus increasing our power density.

The Chariot

A makeshift vehicle to record speed and acceleration.

Recorded Top Speed

349 km/h*

Horsepower

903 hp*

Frame

Chair found in Engineering Block B basement

Weight

40 lbs

Modifications

Drilled a SPAN IGM A1 into the seat

Battery

12 V, 5.6 Ah


This makeshift vehicle dubbed “The Chariot” was made to test the NovAtel equipment we received. It was used as a dummy to setup a Raspberry Pi and automate data collection when powered on. Eventually, we will have to retire The Chariot to mount the SPAN IGM A1 (an accelerometer that records speed and acceleration down to the nanosecond!) on The Zephyr, our electric race bike.

* estimates may be inaccurate
